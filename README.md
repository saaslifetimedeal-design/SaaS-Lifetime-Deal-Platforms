## 🏪 Where to Find Lifetime Deals

The market has diversified beyond one or two dominant players. Each platform has its own approach, and knowing the differences helps you find deals that match your needs.

---

### [Earlybird](https://earlybird.so) — Quality-First Curation

Earlybird takes a fundamentally different approach than most platforms. Instead of listing every available deal, they **curate aggressively**. Not every tool makes the cut, even if the founder offers steep discounts. The focus is on product quality, company viability, and genuine value.

This matters because you spend less time researching and more time building. The curation pre-screens for company sustainability and product-market fit. You're still doing your own due diligence, but you're starting from a higher baseline.

> **Trade-off:** Fewer total deals at any given moment, but the deals you find are more likely to be worth your time. Best for buyers who value their time and want to avoid sorting through mediocre options.

---

### [AppSumo](https://appsumo.com) — The Volume Leader

AppSumo is the biggest name in the space, earning that position through sheer volume. You'll find deals across virtually every category — which is both a strength and a weakness. There are solid products on the platform, but the lighter vetting process means quality varies significantly.

**Worth noting on sustainability:** Because founders typically retain a smaller portion of revenue, some struggle to maintain and grow their product after the deal ends. A noticeable number of tools launched on AppSumo go quiet or shut down within a year or two.

> **Tip:** The review system helps with evaluation, but pay attention to timing. A 4.5-star review from 200 users who bought two days ago tells you less than a 4.0-star review from 2,000 users who've had the product for six months.

---

### [StackSocial](https://www.stacksocial.com) — Beyond Pure SaaS

StackSocial has been around longer than most. They offer both digital and physical products, with daily deals reaching **70% off** for courses and **40% off** for software. The mix of products means software isn't their only focus, so pure SaaS selection is smaller than dedicated platforms.

---

### RocketHub — New Tools and In-House Products

RocketHub specializes in launching brand new tools, including products they've built in-house. That dual role as marketplace and product creator gives them a unique angle, and they've carved out a niche around early-stage discovery.

> **Heads up on pricing:** Deals on RocketHub sometimes carry higher base prices with smaller percentage discounts, meaning the final price can be higher than similar tools on other platforms. Products that launched on RocketHub have also later appeared elsewhere with different pricing structures. Always shop around before committing.

---

### Dealify — The Variety Player

Dealify covers significant ground — from development tools to creative platforms to business software. The diversity is both good and bad: good because you might find unexpected tools, bad because the breadth makes it harder to develop expertise in any category.

> Works well as a **secondary platform** to check alongside a primary one.

---

### DealFuel — Developer and Designer Focus

DealFuel specifically targets **web developers, designers, and entrepreneurs**. Deals skew technical, with emphasis on development tools, design assets, and technical resources.

> If you're building digital products, DealFuel is worth monitoring. Tools here often have features that appeal to developers specifically, not just general business users.

---

### SaaSzilla — ⚠️ One to Be Careful With

SaaSzilla started with promise and looked like it could become a solid player in the lifetime deal space. Unfortunately, the platform has run into serious issues that are hard to overlook.

- Multiple founders have reported **not receiving their payouts**
- Affiliate partners have experienced the same
- **Support has been largely unresponsive** and refund requests have gone unaddressed

> **Recommendation:** Hold off on this platform until there's clear evidence things have improved. When the experience breaks down for sellers, promoters, and buyers alike, it's a sign of deeper problems.

---

### Prime Club — Established Products and Founder Relationships

Prime Club focuses on launching deals for established products and founders they have relationships with. That relationship-driven approach means you're more likely to see tools with **existing user bases and track records**.

**Mixed execution:** Some launches have had confusion around deal terms or communication gaps. There have also been cases where products were *associated* with Prime Club despite not actually launching on the platform, which can confuse the community.

> Prime Club uses **urgency-based promotions** like countdown timers and limited availability windows. Works well for some buyers; others prefer more breathing room to evaluate properly.

---

### Product Canyon — SMB-Focused

Product Canyon targets small and medium-sized businesses with simpler, user-friendly tools. There's not a lot of data available on the platform yet, and they haven't built any meaningful reputation in the space.

> **Caution:** Do your own research carefully before committing to any deal here.

## 🔍 How to Actually Evaluate Lifetime Deals

> Stop buying based on discount percentages. Use this framework to separate legitimate deals from disasters waiting to happen.

---

### 1. Research the Company First

Before testing the product, research the **company**.

| Signal | What to Look For |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Age** | Less than 6 months = higher risk. 1–2 years with a growing user base = safer bet. |
| **Funding** | Angel funding, monthly subscribers, or previous successful exits = more runway. |
| **Roadmap** | Public roadmap = confidence in the future. Silence = concern. |
| **Activity** | Check social media, blog, support channels. Ghost towns suggest founders have moved on. |

Search **LinkedIn** for founders, look them up on **Crunchbase**, and check their **About page**.

---

### 2. Read the Actual Terms Carefully

Lifetime doesn't always mean what you think.

- **What features are included?** Some deals include only current features; future features may be reserved for monthly subscribers.
- **What are the usage limits?** Users, projects, storage, API calls, monthly active users — make sure the deal works for your actual use case.
- **Can you stack codes?** Many platforms let you buy multiple codes to unlock higher tiers. Check before buying whether stacking is allowed and what limits it raises.

---

### 3. Check What Real Users Say

Skip the testimonials on the sales page — those are curated.

- ✅ Read **every review** on the lifetime deal platform. Look for patterns.
- ✅ Search **Reddit** for the product name — people are brutally honest there.
- ✅ Check **Twitter/X** for real-time feedback.
- ✅ Join their **community** if they have one. Active communities with engaged founders are a green flag.

---

### 4. Test Within the Refund Window

> **Pro tip:** Set a calendar reminder for **day 40** of your purchase — giving you a 20-day buffer before the refund window closes.

- Use the product for **real work**, not just clicking around
- Test core features thoroughly
- Contact support with a real question — response time predicts long-term satisfaction more than features do

---

### 5. Calculate True ROI

Everyone shows you the simple math:

```
$29/month × 60 months = $1,740
Lifetime deal: $89 one-time
Savings: $1,651
```

**But ask the harder questions:**
- Will you actually still need this tool in five years?
- Is the company likely to survive that long?
- Does the lifetime tier include what you actually need?

> **Calculate conservatively.** Assume **25–50% of lifetime deals won't make it five years** — the company fails, the tool becomes obsolete, or your needs change. You're still saving thousands compared to subscriptions, just be realistic.

---

## 🚩 Red Flags

Run away fast if you see any of these:

- ❌ Brand new product with zero users *(you're a beta tester who paid for it)*
- ❌ Company selling unlimited lifetime deals with no monthly revenue *(the math doesn't work)*
- ❌ Key features only available to monthly subscribers *(you're second-class)*
- ❌ No product updates in 6+ months *(they've moved on)*
- ❌ Overwhelmingly negative recent reviews *(trust the pattern)*
- ❌ Support taking weeks to respond *(it'll only get worse)*
- ❌ Founder making impossible promises *(disappointment incoming)*

---

## ✅ Green Flags

Buy with confidence when you see:

- ✅ Healthy mix of lifetime **and** monthly subscribers *(monthly revenue funds development)*
- ✅ Regular product updates with detailed changelogs *(transparency matters)*
- ✅ Founder actively engaged in the community *(not outsourced support)*
- ✅ Support responding within **24–48 hours** *(systems are in place)*
- ✅ Growing user base with vocal advocates *(real advocacy beats testimonials)*
- ✅ Clear public product roadmap *(they know where they're going)*
- ✅ Existing funding or revenue beyond lifetime deals *(they have runway)*

---

*Use this guide as a starting point, but always do your own due diligence. The best lifetime deal is one that's still running three years from now.*
